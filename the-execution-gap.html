Operator Insights / The Execution Gap
The Product Operator

Most PE diligence asks whether the company is worth buying. It rarely asks whether the team can deliver the thesis.

Several PE operating partners I work with have lived the same pattern. The deal closed clean. The financial diligence was thorough. The thesis was sound. Six quarters in, the value creation plan is behind, and most of the gap traces back to a product organization that wasn't ready for the plan it was handed. Pre-close diligence rarely tests for that. Post-close, it's the operating partner's problem.

What gets tested vs. what predicts

Diligence is excellent at the things it has always been excellent at.

The financial model gets stress-tested. The pipeline gets validated. The tech stack gets a security review. What rarely gets tested is whether the team running the company has ever delivered against the kind of plan the new owner is about to hand them. The diligence sits on the left. The execution risk lives on the right.

What diligence covers
  • Financial model
  • Customer concentration
  • Churn cohort
  • Sales pipeline
  • Tech stack and security
  • Working capital and unit economics
What diligence rarely covers
  • Product team's track record at the required cadence
  • Whether the roadmap reflects the thesis or the backlog
  • Strength of the CPO seat
  • Discovery rigor in the product process
  • How well the team handles ambiguity
  • Whether the org has shipped strategic shifts before

The thesis is on the left. The risk is on the right.

A value creation plan behind by two quarters carries multiples of the original deal premium in delayed return. None of it traces back to anything that was tested at diligence.
Six questions

The questions that surface execution risk before close.

These are diligence questions, not interview questions. They go to the deal team, the operating partner, and the candidate company's leadership. Six questions whose honest answers usually predict execution risk faster than any other piece of pre-close work. None of them require proprietary access. All of them are routinely skipped.

01

"Has this team ever shipped a strategic shift before?"

The deal thesis often requires the product org to shift focus from what it does today to something different. A team that has only ever executed continuity may not be capable of discontinuity.

What this surfaces An org that built its current product on a strong product-led-growth motion may not be capable of executing the enterprise-sales-driven roadmap the thesis depends on.
02

"Does the roadmap match the thesis, or the backlog?"

Most product roadmaps reflect what the team has been building. The investment thesis often depends on what the team should be building next. Those are rarely the same document.

What this surfaces A roadmap dominated by reliability and tech-debt work may indicate engineering is fighting fires, not executing growth bets.
03

"How is the CPO seat?"

An empty CPO seat, an interim CPO, or a CPO promoted because nobody else was available is a different risk profile than a strong operator in the role. Diligence usually doesn't surface which one is in place.

What this surfaces A founder-CEO acting as their own CPO is a known risk pattern. Diligence rarely names it.
04

"What's the discovery cadence?"

The investment thesis depends on the product team understanding the customer well enough to make the right calls. A team without a structured discovery practice is making guesses, not informed decisions.

What this surfaces A product org running zero customer interviews per quarter cannot validate the assumptions the thesis depends on.
05

"How does the team handle ambiguity?"

Value creation plans always require decisions under incomplete information. A team that defaults to consensus or to executive direction will move slowly. A team with strong operating principles will move faster.

What this surfaces An org where every roadmap decision routes through the CEO is bottlenecked. The CEO becomes the constraint on execution speed.
06

"What's the relationship between product and sales?"

If product and sales are at war, the value creation plan will be litigated quarterly instead of executed. Diligence often misses this because both parties speak well of each other in interviews.

What this surfaces A roadmap with no sales-influenced items, or one entirely dictated by sales, are both warning signs.
Worked example

A deal that closed clean and underperformed.

Setup

A growth-stage SaaS company gets acquired. The thesis: shift from a self-serve, mid-market product to an enterprise platform with a strategic sales motion. The financial diligence is clean. The customer concentration is acceptable. The product team is praised in management interviews.

Outcome, eighteen months later

Enterprise pipeline is at 30 percent of plan. The product team has been working on the roadmap, but the roadmap still skews toward the self-serve user. The CPO was promoted from senior product manager during the deal process. They've never shipped an enterprise platform before. None of this was untrue at diligence. None of it was asked.

Pattern based on common post-close findings, not a single sourced case.

Diagnostic

Six questions for the deal team.

Yes or no, asked of the deal team or the candidate company's leadership during pre-close. Two questions an operating partner can run on a Friday afternoon, four questions a CPO advisor can answer in a 90-minute working session. The questions are blunt by design. Hedging defeats the point. If the honest answer is yes-but, it's a no.

  1. Has the product organization ever shipped a strategic shift of similar magnitude to the one the thesis requires?
  2. Does the current product roadmap reflect the deal thesis, or does it reflect the team's existing backlog?
  3. Is the CPO seat held by a strong operator, or is it vacant, interim, or recently filled from within?
  4. Does the team have a documented discovery cadence, with evidence of customer interviews driving decisions in the last two quarters?
  5. Does the org have documented operating principles for decisions made under ambiguity, or do most decisions route to the CEO?
  6. Can the team produce evidence that product and sales are aligned on the next 18 months of priorities?
Five or six yeses: the team is likely to deliver. Three or four: there's execution risk that needs to be priced into the plan. Fewer than three: the thesis depends on a team that hasn't demonstrated it can execute it.
What an audit produces

Output the deal team can act on.

Execution-readiness diligence is not a leadership review. It's a structured assessment that names whether the existing team can deliver the value creation plan, where the gaps are, and what they cost. Three things define the output.

A documented assessment of the product organization's track record against the cadence the thesis requires.

The team's prior strategic shifts get named, dated, and rated. The cadence required by the thesis gets compared against the cadence the org has actually delivered. Gaps surface as a track-record finding, not a gut call.

A roadmap-to-thesis fit analysis that names what the team is building and what the thesis requires them to build.

Each item on the current roadmap gets tagged: thesis-aligned, neutral, or backlog-driven. The mismatch is quantified. The deal team sees what would have to change in the first hundred days for the plan to be on track.

A specific list of execution risks priced into the operating model, not assumed away.

Each risk has an owner, a cost, and a mitigation path. The list lands in the IC memo and the operating model, not in a separate appendix. Execution risk becomes a number the deal team can defend rather than a vibe.
