Operator Insights / The Drift Problem
The Product Operator

The metric the AI trained on may not mean what it means today.

Several PE diligence teams I work with run thorough data audits before greenlighting an AI investment. Most of those audits look at what's in the records: typos, nulls, contamination, missing fields. They rarely look at what the records mean. That's where schema drift lives, and it's the failure mode most likely to break an AI investment thesis quietly.

The drift

One column. Five years. Three different metrics.

Same column name. Same dashboard label. Three different definitions stacked into one time series. Each shift had a defensible reason. None got recorded where a downstream model could see it.

2020

monthly_active_users = users with any session in the last 30 days.

Triggered by First instrumentation. The team picked the loosest credible definition because something needed to ship by quarter-end.
2022

monthly_active_users = users with three or more sessions in the last 30 days.

Triggered by Analytics audit. One-session events and tab-opens were inflating the count. The definition tightened. History stayed loose.
2024

monthly_active_users = users who completed a defined "core action" in the last 30 days.

Triggered by Product strategy shift. Engagement defined by outcomes, not visits. Definition rewritten end-to-end. History never reconciled.
What the model sees

Five years of one column read as one time series. Three patterns absorbed as features. The model can't see that the column's meaning shifted, and predicts confidently against an incoherent input.

Five years of monthly_active_users. Three different definitions. The chart shows growth. Some of it is real. Some of it is definitional.

Why drift hides

Drift is invisible to almost everyone who could catch it.

The reason drift slips through diligence is that nobody whose job it is to catch it has the full view. Each stakeholder sees a piece. The piece looks consistent. The mismatch lives in the gaps between them.

The data team. They may know the definition changed because they made the change themselves. The change was discussed in standup, scoped in a ticket, and shipped. It was not always documented in a place a downstream consumer can find. The change is local to the team that made it.

The model. The model sees a column name and a value. It cannot see the metadata that would tell it the value's definition shifted in 2022 and again in 2024. Five years of training data look like one continuous time series. The model trains on it accordingly and learns three patterns at once.

The board. The metric on the board deck has the same name today as it did three years ago. The trend chart looks coherent. The board doesn't know to ask whether the underlying definition stayed stable, and the operating team that presents the deck doesn't always know to flag it. The conversation moves on.

The AI investment thesis. The thesis depends on a growth pattern in a specific metric. Some of that growth is real customer behavior changing over time. Some of it is definitional shifts the metric absorbed quietly. The thesis cannot tell the difference. The model trained to act on the thesis cannot either.

Drift is a coordination problem disguised as a data problem.

Causes

Four ways drift gets introduced.

Drift isn't malicious and it isn't a sign of a sloppy data team. Most of the time, it's the byproduct of getting better at measuring something while leaving history alone. Four common patterns surface in the audits I run, often in combination on the same metric.

01
Product evolution
The product changed. The metric had to.

The thing the metric measures genuinely changed because the product changed. New features, new user behaviors, new core actions. The old definition no longer fits.

In real systems A "session" meant a tab open in 2020. By 2024, with native mobile and offline sync, "session" was redefined to cover something else entirely.
02
Analytics maturity
The team got better. History didn't catch up.

The data team got better at measuring the same thing. The new definition is more rigorous. Historical data wasn't backfilled to the new definition.

In real systems The team realized "active users" shouldn't include accounts whose only activity was a webhook firing. The definition tightened. Historical numbers stayed loose.
03
Cross-system reconciliation
Two systems disagreed. One won.

Two systems disagreed about the same metric. Someone picked one definition and called it canonical. The other system's history quietly stopped mattering.

In real systems Salesforce reported 4,200 customers. The data warehouse reported 4,540. The team standardized on the warehouse number. The CRM history became inconsistent with itself.
04
Definitional debt
Loose at first. Tightened in the dark.

The metric was loosely defined when first instrumented. As the team got more sophisticated, the definition tightened informally without being version-controlled.

In real systems Three different engineers each had their own implicit definition of "churn." Reports that combined their queries gave different answers depending on who ran them.
Worked example

The metric that drifted under the AI bet.

A growth-stage SaaS company is being evaluated for acquisition. The investment thesis depends on continued growth in monthly active users, which has compounded at 47 percent annually for three years. The diligence team validates the trend, validates the methodology for the current period, and signs off. The deal closes.

Eighteen months later, the AI churn-prediction model trained on five years of MAU history is producing predictions the operating team can't reconcile with what they're seeing on the ground. The model isn't wrong. The MAU definition shifted twice during the training window. The model learned three different patterns at once and can't untangle them.

Pattern based on common diligence findings, not a single sourced study.

Diagnostic

Six questions.

Yes or no for the metrics your AI investment depends on. Two questions per stakeholder layer. Take it as an operating partner running diligence on a portfolio company, or as a CDO answering for your own org. The questions are blunt by design. Hedging defeats the point. If the honest answer is yes-but, it's a no.

Layer 01 · data team
  1. For the metrics your AI investment thesis depends on, can your data team produce a documented change history of the field's definition over the last 24 months?
  2. When a metric definition changes, is the change recorded somewhere a downstream model can reference, or is it tribal knowledge?
Layer 02 · downstream consumers
  1. When the board sees a five-year trend chart, does anyone validate that the metric definition stayed stable across that window?
  2. Do the dashboards your operating team uses display the metric definition alongside the metric value, or just the value?
Layer 03 · investment thesis
  1. Does your AI investment thesis depend on growth in a specific metric over a specific time window? If yes, has anyone audited that metric's definition history across that window?
  2. If the metric drifted during the training window, would your AI model's outputs reveal that, or would they silently absorb it as signal?
Five or six yeses: drift is unlikely to break the thesis. Three or four: there's drift exposure that's not currently quantified. Fewer than three: the AI investment thesis is built on a metric that may not have meant the same thing throughout its history.
What an audit produces

Output that names the drift exposure.

A drift-aware audit isn't a hygiene report and it isn't a slide deck of definitional observations. It surfaces drift quantitatively, ties each instance to the part of the investment thesis it puts at risk, and produces artifacts the AI training pipeline can read directly. Three things define the output.

A documented change history for each metric the AI investment depends on, including the date and reason for each definition change.

Every change has a date, an owner, a reason, and the version of the metric before and after. The history sits next to the metric in the data dictionary, not in a Slack thread or a PR description nobody can find later.

A model-readable artifact (typically a YAML or JSON schema with versioned definitions) that downstream AI systems can consume to detect drift at training time.

The schema is checked into the same repo as the model code. When the schema version changes, the training pipeline either fails or flags the affected window. Drift becomes a build-time signal, not a six-month operational mystery.

A drift-exposure score for the investment thesis, naming which metrics are stable enough to train on and which are not.

Each metric gets a stability rating across the training window. Metrics with high drift get sequestered or excluded. The score lands in the IC memo and the post-close remediation plan, not in a technical appendix the deal team won't open.
