Operator Insights / The Data Bottleneck
The Product Operator

Most AI investments get evaluated on the model. The data underneath is often where the failure actually lives.

Several PE diligence teams I work with run thorough AI readiness assessments. They evaluate tooling. They evaluate vendors. They evaluate use-case selection. The one thing they often don't evaluate is whether the data the AI will run on is fit for the AI to run on. The investment thesis depends on that data. The diligence rarely tests it.

What gets graded vs. what predicts

The diligence is on the visible layer. The risk is one layer down.

The standard AI readiness assessment is a thorough piece of work. It surfaces real gaps in tooling, talent, and use-case selection, and the recommendations are usually correct. The problem is what it doesn't ask. The investment thesis depends on a model running on production data. The assessment rarely tests whether that data is in a state to be run on.

What readiness assessments tend to grade
  • Tool licenses and vendor selection
  • Use-case identification
  • Talent and AI fluency
  • Workflow integration plans
  • Budget and timeline
What predicts whether the AI bet works
  • Production data fit for the model's job
  • Schema definitions stable enough to train on
  • Coverage across the segments the thesis depends on
  • Source-of-truth alignment across systems
  • Evidence that contamination has been audited and removed

Five failure modes

The patterns that make production data unfit for AI.

Across the diligence engagements I run, the same five patterns surface in some combination almost every time. None of them appear on a standard readiness checklist. Each one, on its own, is enough to make a model's output unreliable in ways that don't get caught until the AI is in production and the metric the thesis depends on stops moving.

01
Contamination
Synthetic records inside production.

Synthetic, demo, and QA records living inside production tables. The AI trains on a dataset where a meaningful percentage isn't real customer activity at all. I find this in nearly every audit.

Consequence Models learn patterns from noise. The growth story reads as customer traction when it's pipeline artifacts.
Read more

I've seen production tables where 40 to 60 percent of records originated as test or demo data. The fix order: build a documented rule that flags synthetic records, run it against historical tables, then decide what to retroactively exclude before any AI training run.

02
Automation artifacts
System-generated activity that reads as user behavior.

System-generated records that look indistinguishable from user behavior. Webhooks, scheduled jobs, automated email opens, integration backfills. They aren't malicious. They're instrumentation nobody labeled.

Consequence Engagement metrics inflate. Churn signals dampen. The model learns from things that aren't users at all.
Read more

The pattern is most severe in product analytics tables. Typical findings: integration backfills inflate session counts, webhook events double-count user actions, scheduled jobs simulate engagement on dormant accounts. Each one shifts the model's reading of what active looks like.

03
Schema drift
Field meanings that quietly changed.

Field meanings that shifted over time without documentation. The same column name carries three different definitions across five years of history. The chart still ticks up.

Consequence Time-series analysis becomes incoherent. The model is trained on a metric that doesn't mean what the team thinks it means.
Read more

Look at any field your investment thesis depends on and ask: what was the definition two years ago? If the answer requires reading old PR descriptions or Slack threads, the field is drifted. The fix is a definition log that downstream consumers can reference.

04
Coverage gaps
Segments the data doesn't represent.

Customer segments, time periods, geographies, or product lines missing from the dataset entirely. The data doesn't represent the population the AI is supposed to serve. The model never knows it's missing.

Consequence Models inherit blind spots by design. The AI works on the segments it has data for and fails silently on the segments it doesn't.
Read more

Coverage gaps don't announce themselves. The model returns confident outputs on every input, including segments it has never seen. Audit by listing the segments, time windows, and product lines that drove the training data, then comparing against the population the model is asked to serve.

05
Source-of-truth conflicts
Same entity, different definitions.

The same entity represented differently in two systems with no reconciliation layer. Customer A in Salesforce is Customer A' in the data warehouse. Each downstream consumer picks one and moves on.

Consequence Any AI built on top inherits the conflict. The output is internally inconsistent in ways that compound across analyses.
Read more

Common version: Salesforce treats Account as the customer entity, the data warehouse rolls up to a parent organization, the marketing platform keys on email domain. None of them agree. Any model that joins across them inherits whichever choice the analyst happened to make that day.

Worked example

Same metric. Three definitions.

Schema drift is the most universally recognizable of the five because every product team has lived through at least one quiet definition change. The column name stays. The definition shifts. Nobody updates the dashboard label, and the chart keeps climbing. Here's what it looks like for a single field, monthly_active_users, across five years of history. The column name never changed. The meaning did, twice.

2020

monthly_active_users = users with any session in the last 30 days.

2022

monthly_active_users = users with three or more sessions in the last 30 days.

2024

monthly_active_users = users who completed a defined "core action" in the last 30 days.

Same metric name. Three different definitions. The AI investment thesis depends on growth in this metric. The growth in the chart is partly real, partly definitional.

A churn-prediction model trained on five years of monthly_active_users history learns three different patterns at once. The model's predictions aren't wrong because the model is bad. They're wrong because the input is incoherent.

Pattern based on common diligence findings, not a single sourced study.

Diagnostic

Six questions.

Yes or no for the data your AI investment depends on. Two questions per layer. Take it as an operating partner running diligence on a portfolio company, or as a CTO answering for your own org. The questions are blunt by design. Hedging defeats the point. If the honest answer is yes-but, it's a no.

Layer 01 · contamination and artifacts
  1. Do you know what percentage of records in your production tables are real customer activity vs. demo, test, or system-generated?
  2. Has anyone written a documented rule for distinguishing real customer events from automation artifacts?
Layer 02 · schema and definitions
  1. For the metrics your AI investment thesis depends on, can you produce the schema definition history for the last 24 months?
  2. When a field definition changes, is the change recorded somewhere a downstream model can reference?
Layer 03 · coverage and source of truth
  1. For the customer segments the AI investment depends on, what's the coverage rate in the underlying data?
  2. When the same entity appears in two systems with conflicting definitions, is there a documented reconciliation rule, or do downstream consumers each pick one?
Five or six yeses: the data is fit for the AI investment. Three or four: the gap is real and the AI bet has unmeasured risk. Fewer than three: the AI investment is being made on data that hasn't been audited.
What an audit produces

Output an investment committee can act on.

A data-fit-for-AI audit isn't a slide deck of data hygiene observations. It is a forensic finding linked back to the investment thesis, sequenced by what changes the bet, and shaped to drop into the documents the decision is being made in. The deliverable belongs at the IC table, not in a separate technical report nobody reads. Three things define the output.

Findings linked to source records, with confidence rated on every claim.

Every finding traces back to specific rows, fields, or query results. Confidence is rated explicitly. No claim is made without the evidence that supports it sitting next to it, ready to be inspected by the IC or the deal team.

Recommendations sequenced as fix / spend / defer, mapped to the investment thesis.

Each recommendation is tagged with what it costs, what it protects, and which part of the thesis it supports. The output tells the deal team what has to be done before close, what gets capital post-close, and what can wait.

Output that drops into an IC memo, a board deck, or a remediation plan without rewriting.

The artifact is shaped for where it goes next. A page in the IC memo. A slide in the board deck. A remediation plan handed to the portfolio CTO. No translation step. No second draft. The format is the deliverable.
