You paid for the AI. Nobody built the system.

Several product orgs I work with have the same problem. AI spend is on the P&L. Measurable operational outcomes aren't in the data. Here's what's causing it.

For operating partners

The spend is visible. The return isn't.

When a PE operating partner asks a portfolio company to justify its AI investment, the conversation usually ends the same way. Tool licenses, seats, training spend: all documented. PM cycle time, rework rate, handoff failure rate, time-to-release: none of it tracked. The investment has no defensible return because no one built the system that would generate one. This isn't unique to one company. It's the default state of AI adoption across the market. Tools deployed. Workflows unchanged. P&L impacted. EBITDA story missing.

Gap 01 · Most common

The AI doesn't know who you are.

You've given the most capable reasoning system ever built zero grounding in your company. No product. No customer. No market. No strategic priorities. Every prompt starts from scratch. The output feels generic because it is. The AI isn't being lazy. It's working with nothing.

What it costs

40–60% of PM output requires significant rework before it can be used. At a loaded PM cost, that's not a productivity gap. It's a margin problem. The org is paying for AI and still paying for the rework.

"We ran it three times and kept getting the same boilerplate. We might as well have Googled it."
Gap 02 · Most expensive

The work doesn't survive the handoff.

The AI doesn't know where the output goes, so it gives you a wall of text when you needed a development-ready spec. But even when the format is right, the reasoning behind it rarely travels. Someone summarizes it in Slack. Someone else interprets the summary in a ticket. By the time it reaches the person doing the work, the original intent is three degrees removed. Two failure modes, same result: the work arrives broken.

What it costs

A bad spec isn't one hour of rework. It's one sprint. Misaligned execution doesn't show up in your AI budget. It shows up in cycle time, rework rate, and missed release dates.

Gap 03 · Most overlooked

Everything is there. Nothing is decided.

The AI doesn't know what you're trying to decide, what tradeoffs matter, or who's reading the output. So it gives you comprehensive. Comprehensive isn't actionable. You still have to do the hard part. This is the gap that makes AI feel smart but not useful. When outputs don't accelerate decisions, AI added a step. It didn't remove one.

What it costs

Decision latency is the drag nobody tracks. Every cycle where AI produces output but doesn't sharpen a decision is cost absorbed with no value captured.

"The brief was thorough. I read it twice and still didn't know what we were recommending."
