Operator Insights / The Political Roadmap
The Product Operator

A product roadmap is a political document. The team that pretends otherwise is the team that ships the wrong things.

Several product organizations I work with run a quarterly roadmap review that turns into a negotiation. Engineering wants more capacity for tech debt. Sales wants commitments to specific quarters. The CEO wants a clearer narrative. The board wants evidence of strategic discipline. The roadmap leaving the meeting is shaped by whoever pushed hardest in the room. The team thinks they have a roadmap. They have a compromise.

Four readers

One source of truth. Four documents.

The four roadmaps the org needs are not four versions of the same thing. They are four artifacts at four altitudes, each shaped for the reader who has to act on it. Most product orgs write one document and call it four.

The mismatch

Why one roadmap can't serve four readers.

When a single roadmap is asked to do four jobs, every reader compromises and nobody is served. Each reader is reading the document at a different altitude, with a different question in mind, and the document can only be optimized for one of them at a time.

Engineering. Needs capacity-bound work, near-term, with dependencies and unknowns surfaced. The unified roadmap softens technical reality to keep sales and the board comfortable. The cost: engineering plans against a roadmap that can't be honored, surfaces the gap halfway through the quarter, and gets blamed for the slip.

Sales. Needs themes they can speak about confidently to customers, without specific commitment dates that will be missed. The unified roadmap forces them to choose between vagueness, which loses deals, and overcommitment, which loses customers later. They pick the path that closes this quarter and deal with next quarter when it arrives.

The CEO. Needs a strategic narrative that maps to the company's stated priorities. The unified roadmap gets cluttered with operational detail engineering and sales need. By the time the CEO is presenting it to the executive team, the strategic story has been buried under quarterly commitments and dependency notes.

The board. Needs evidence that the team is executing the value creation plan. The unified roadmap is too granular to read at the board's altitude and too sanitized to surface real risk. The board sees a polished artifact, doesn't ask the right questions, and finds out about the gap two quarters later.

Each reader is reading a different document, then arguing about why their document doesn't match yours.

The four roadmaps

What each artifact is actually for.

Same priorities. Four different artifacts. Each authored at the altitude its reader needs, in the format they can act on. None of them tries to be the others. The work isn't writing four documents from scratch every quarter. It's keeping one source of priorities clean, then deriving each artifact from it deliberately.

Worked example

The roadmap review that turned into a fight.

Setup

A CPO walks into a quarterly roadmap review with a single 18-month roadmap. Engineering pushes back: too much committed, no capacity for the work that's actually needed to keep the platform stable. Sales pushes back: nothing they can take to customers without overcommitting. The CEO pushes back: where's the strategic story? The CPO leaves the meeting with a roadmap that's been edited four times by people with conflicting needs.

Outcome, six weeks later

The roadmap that ships is the artifact of the meeting, not the artifact of strategy. Engineering deprioritizes work that wasn't surfaced. Sales accidentally commits to a date that wasn't supposed to be a commitment. The CEO can't explain the narrative to the board. The CPO is the visible failure point. The actual failure point is structural. One document was asked to do four jobs.

Pattern based on common product-org dynamics, not a single sourced case.

Diagnostic

Six questions.

Yes or no for your own product organization. Take it as a CPO answering for yourself, or as a CEO watching the roadmap review from the outside. The questions are blunt by design. Hedging defeats the point. If the honest answer is yes-but, it's a no.

  1. Does your product organization produce a single roadmap document that's shown to engineering, sales, the CEO, and the board?
  2. When that roadmap is reviewed, do meeting participants argue about what specific items mean: what level of commitment, what timeline, what scope?
  3. Has the roadmap been edited in the last quarter to reflect a complaint from a specific stakeholder, rather than a change in actual priorities?
  4. Do customer-facing teams have a roadmap they can show customers without legal review?
  5. Does engineering have a capacity-bound view of the next two quarters that's distinct from the document the board sees?
  6. When the board asks about strategic shifts, can the team produce a roadmap framed at the board's altitude without rebuilding it from scratch?
Five or six yeses: the org has specialized its roadmaps. Three or four: the roadmap is doing too much work. Fewer than three: the roadmap is the source of the political fight, not the resolution to it.
What specialization looks like

One source. Four artifacts. A discipline that holds them in sync.

Specialization isn't four uncoordinated documents. It's a single source of priorities, owned by the CPO, that propagates into four artifacts, each shaped for its reader. Three things define the discipline.

A single source of priorities, owned by the CPO, that all four roadmaps derive from.

The priorities live in one document with one owner. When they change, the change is visible. The four roadmaps regenerate from the same source, so they cannot drift from each other or from the company's actual direction.

Four artifacts, each authored for a specific reader, each consistent with the source priorities, each different in format and altitude.

The strategic roadmap is in themes. The engineering roadmap is in capacity blocks. The sales roadmap is in quarterly stories. The board roadmap is a three-year arc. Same priorities, four formats. None of them tries to be the others.

A discipline of regenerating the four artifacts when priorities change, instead of editing one document four times to satisfy four stakeholders.

When priorities shift, the source updates first, then each artifact gets regenerated. Stakeholders react to consistent translations of the same decision rather than fighting over edits to a single document that was never going to satisfy them.
